#InThisTogether

If there’s one thing the past year has taught us is that the support of one another is extremely important. When we’re #InThisTogether, we can overcome even the most difficult of times. During the pandemic, it was important to have a support system for our employees and their families. It was also necessary to have a forum for employees to come together and share their experiences.

#InThisTogether we have provided the best support wherever possible, here are a few highlights:

  • 50+ Volunteers formed the Covid-19 Support Group to assist COVID struck employees/family members across India
  • Surveys are conducted to understand the overall picture and extend support to the urgent needs
  • A small team of Volunteers lead the topics like ambulances, labs, hospital connections etc.
  • 1760 employees and family members vaccinated at the company site
  • #MyCowinStory - Positivity Podcasts by employees who bravely overcame COVID inflicted illness.
  • These podcasts are available on Yammer at “MyCoWinStory Podcast”
  • Leadership connects, response & ongoing support to ensure calm & hope is maintained amongst all team members.

From a company perspective, #InThisTogether is much more than a support group. It is an effort to help our employees during a ‘once-in-a-century’ pandemic. One of the key efforts of this initiative was to provide vaccination to our employees. As we all know by now, getting vaccinated is important for us to get out of this pandemic as soon as possible.

To ensure that our employees have access to the vaccine, we launched a vaccination drive in early 2021. This COVID-19 vaccination drive covered 20+ cities all over the country and we were able to vaccinate 13,000 people in a short span of time! In Pune itself 1760 of our employees were able to get the vaccine easily.

All this has been made possible because of the efforts of our volunteers and the support of our employees who cooperated to make the vaccination drive a success.

Another area of focus of our #InThisTogether initiative was to bring our employees closer together. With the physical offices closed because of the pandemic and social distancing, social isolation was a big hurdle to overcome. To top it off, we were surrounded by negative news and events during the peak of the second wave in early 2021.

To combat this, we launched the #MyCowinStory podcast. These positivity podcasts featured SIEMENS employees who overcame COVID-19 and shared their stories with us. We got a chance to hear how they fought isolation, illness, and negative thoughts with the help of their family, loved ones and sheer resilience.

Such stories inspired everyone and reinforced a sense of positivity and morale during a very difficult time for most. We are thankful to everyone who shared their experiences with us and also to those who tuned in to the podcasts. While the pandemic may be ongoing, it is important to remember that we are #InThisTogether and with the right support we can tide over the tough times soon.

Understanding Excellence

Dr. Anand Nadkarni,
MBBS-General Medicine, MD-Psychiatry,
DPM-Psychology, Founder - Institute for Psychological Health (IPH)

I was facilitating an interactive session with school going students under the banner of ‘Shikshak Prab- hodhini’ – a project where teachers are being trained to mentor exceptional students. We were discussing ‘emotions’. A young girl of eighth standard, an aspiring singer, was discussing with me her emotions before a performance, in front of about 80 other students. We talked about the ‘Result’ and the ‘Process’.

The ‘result’ was the end of the performance when the audience would clap and appreciate. The ‘Process’ was the actual performance that would lead to this result.

We concluded that before starting the actual performance we need to make the ‘result’ ‘out of focus’ that is blurred and bring the actual performance in sharp focus ... by sharp focus we meant, we will now focus on every minute of the performance... minute by minute. The girl visualized aloud the exact moment of starting the performance. She described it as if in a trance, I was helping her to shape her responses ... Here she was, as steady as a rock ... breathing rhythmically.. Image of her guru in front of her eyes ... and the only overriding thought, I am going to do my best...

“And who are you? ... what are you? ... at this moment? ...”

“I am music ...” pat came the reply

“A moment of excellence is born” I said.

It was a very strong experience emotionally for all of us present. A rare, delightful insight into an experience called ‘excellence’ ... It was exhilarating in an enlightened manner. At times, the experiential truth is so strong that is difficult to catch it in words, however, if one has to consolidate the emotional insight into a blinding design of wisdom, one has to fall back on words again.

So what is excellence?

I do not think excellence has one single definition. In the future segments of this communication, I will explore different ways by which this theme can be understood. Here is the first one.

Let us take for example, a popular skill amongst students such as ‘orator’s skill’. Translated in school-reality, it means participating and winning elocution and debating competitions.

A student that is generally chosen by teachers for developing this skill is an expand communicative one who also has a ‘pleasant’ and ‘smart ‘disposition’. Suppose there is an elocution competition and five topics are given, out of which she has to select one.

The mentor here will ask her to think and talk about each one of the topics... what are the thoughts that come to her mind when she reads topics such as , ‘Protecting My Earth’ or ‘The Life and Times of Shivaji Maharaj’ . She is encouraged to explore each topic; in her own way. She has to just go on talking about each topic. The mentor listens keenly.

“Can you now make a choice?” the mentor asks the question unconstructively. She needs to give her own explanation why she is deciding to opt for a particular topic. If she is confused, the mentor helps her to explore her confusion (please note, not to clear it).

When she makes a choice she has to give justification for it. Having heard that; the mentor poses certain relevant queries regarding her choice. What is the most important aspect of the topic that she has liked? What according to her is the part of the topic that she may find difficult? If she has ‘liked’ two topics, what is one consideration that will help her to choose the one that she has chosen?

At the end, the mentor and student will decide a time interval where the student has to think of all the points raised and come back with a decision.

“But sir, I have already made up my mind” suppose the student says.

“Yes, I appreciate ... I want you now to tell me one by one why and how you discarded the other topics. I am interested in knowing how you did it...”

“How did I do it? I just did it” student. “Let us explore how you could have done it... I am taking a paper and writing about all topics. I want you to write all the thoughts that came to your mind ... that could help ...”

The mentor helps the student to identify consciously the thinking process that went behind her decision. The topic is now selected.

The mentor now motivates the student to gather information on the topic. The mentor shares the spirit of enquiry, exploration and excitement.

Having an adequate ‘database’ the mentor helps the student to evolve the structure of presentation. The structure is not imposed. Any deviation from standard procedure of delivering a speech, is respected and discussed. The mundane use of quotations, use of lofty words, adult and literary phrases is questioned. The originality of expression is encouraged. A draft is now ready.

The mentor and the student now discuss the style of presentation. The mentor does not impose his or any other’s style on the student. They make a list of ‘impressive orators’ that they have observed in media as well as in public life. The student makes note of the qualities that she would like to imbibe from each one of them. The written draft is taken as a guideline rather than a script.

While rehearsing the speech, placement of all phrases and points is discussed. The contents are rearranged, realigned and the impact is noted. Then the student is given different scenarios and told to present the speech

  • Student is the first speaker
  • Last speaker
  • All the points covered by the previous speaker
  • Distracted audience
  • Tired judges
  • Interruption because of electricity / technical faults.

Each performance is discussed.

Then the ‘emotions’ during preparation and rehearsal are discussed. Boredom, anxiety, satisfaction...

Each emotion is named and each emotion is acknowledged. The emotions that will help the performance are identified. How can the student continue to generate these helpful emotions? ... different strategies are planned by which this can be done.

The issue of ‘ownership’ of performance is now discussed. This ‘ownership’ can be burdening or enjoyable, the way you look at it.

On the day of the performance, the mentor and the student spend a short ‘exclusive time’ together. After the performance the mentor and the student discuss pros and cons of her own performance. Rather than saying “your delivery was too fast”, it is put as “These were the sentences where your speed was fast” Rather than saying “you need to work on your eye-to-eye contact” it is put as, “you need to periodically cover all corners of the audience by your eyes”.

After the performance is discussed, both mentor and student sincerely sit through some other contestants’ speeches and discuss those.

Slowly they evolve a detailed format of performance appraisal which is kept as a record by the student. After the result of the competition, the mentor explores resultant emotions with the student. They decide on their next goal.

As the time progresses, the student starts becoming more responsible and empowered and the mentor maintains a meaningful presence throughout this journey.

The student now grows confident and starts exploring the experience further. The mentor now focuses on ‘involvement’, ‘commitment’, ‘process satisfaction’ and ‘result satisfaction’ which are psychologically more sophisticated terms.

The discussion revolves around, what is the ‘self-talk’ at such times and how different types of self-talk can be either helpful or harmful; how the student can exercise her choice to choose the most helpful self-talk.

As the string of performances continues and each is utilized as a ‘learning’ experience, there will come an experience the mentor is watching for... a memorable moment when the student and the speech being delivered becomes so synchronized that it becomes ONE.

The student is at her best at that moment. This moment is again shared and explored by both. It is a magical moment. A touch of brilliance. Now, the mentor and the student keep on working further to make this moment appear often as well as how they can make it ‘better’. Here better, means more meaningful, more memorable, more intense…

And slowly, the moments of excellence start trickling... then flowing... Naturally ... In a rhythm. The student has discovered her own way of expression.

The mentor watches with his ever watchful eyes her track record of glories with moist eyes, with all the memories gathering one by one in his mind... “There was a day when I helped her to make her choice” he says in his mind and smiles.

End of Train Telemetry

Harsh Piparsaniya

Ever wondered, how, many bogies of a Train come to a halt at the same time? The supply of pneumatic pressure from the Air generation unit to bogies at the end introduces significant delay in application of brakes. Having a Brake Control Unit in the Braking system to manage the braking of the bogies to stop them simultaneously could be one of the answers.

However, this would require revamping the existing brake system on the trains to support the Brake Control Unit – a massive logistical nightmare for any Rail Network Provider. The situation is pitiful incase of Freight trains which stretch over length of kms, requiring Railway guards to apply the brakes. This is exhaustive, unreliable and could turn fateful on Emergency Applications of brakes.

Solution to this problem requires a modular control system that applies the brakes on the rear of the train with no manual intervention by constant consultation with Locomotive. EOTT End of Train Telemetry, the latest offering from Siemens is a precise fit of the Solution.

The EOTT system mainly consists of Head of Train (HOT) fitted in locomotives and End of Train (EOT) fitted on the last vehicle of the train. EOTT has Global Positioning System (GPS) module to indicate location of the train and Global System for Mobile (GSM) communication module to transfer data to server besides the radio device for communication and control between HOT and EOT.

High visibility marker light flasher devices in EOT have been provided with automatic switching on and off based on the ambient light condition. The brake power pressure of the last vehicle of the train is displayed to the train driver in the HOT display in the cab of the locomotive.

The driver can confirm brake power pressure drop in the train up to the last vehicle during application of air brake by observing brake power pressure of the last vehicle remotely in the HOT display.

The system allows the emergency brake to be applied from the last vehicle of the train by opening a valve in EOT remotely from the cab of the locomotive. It has the provision for automatic application of train emergency air brakes also from rear when emergency train air brake is applied by loco pilot in the cab and the simultaneous application of brake from front and rear will reduce the braking distance and time for controlling the train.

In case of train parting confirmed by the loco pilot, an emergency brake from EOT, if required by train driver can be applied in order to bring the rear portion to an early halt. The available provisions will boost the confidence of loco pilots during running of trains with EOTT.

Head of Train

Some of the key features of EOTT:

  • AIR Generator to charge battery.
  • A push button to allow user to perform operations like ARM(establish a connection between EOT and HOT)
  • GPS receiver for location ,motion and speed detection of EOT.
  • HVM (high visibility marker) which glows in dark conditions to guide the device presence to the rear train.
  • EOT also reports the status to the central server and status includes the rear air pressure, current GPS location, battery status, battery temperature, air generator voltage with different EOT states like wake, Deployed, Emergency ,sleep etc.
  • Both EOT and HOT communicate over RF communication as per are AAR guidelines to make sure the EOT is in good state.

Currently EOTT is working on an advanced platform-based product with Software defined Radio Concept on HOT part. EOTT has been successfully demonstrated on East Coast Railway, which became the first division of Indian Railways to adopt EOTT.

Positive Train Control (PTC)

Divyesh Patel

Problem/Background

A railway network is a guided system made up of controlled access and exit points. To avoid train cars from getting lost, switches guide the rail-cars along the right tracks to get them from a given access point to the exit point where their journey ends.

Rail networks are under someone’s control, and they’re highly connected. These connections can be represented using different mapping methods: geographic, schematic, linear or 3D, each of which is suited to specific purposes. The geographic database plays a vital role in order to represent the railway network in the form of schematic, linear or 3D as it provides the actual physical view of various railway network topologies and its infrastructure.

In PTC(Positive Train Control) project, we are working on Track Schematic module, It takes care of track layout representation wherein railway tracks are drawn horizontally across the drawing canvas on HMI, this helps a locomotive pilot to understand the current location of a train,

routes ahead and around the train and constraints(speed profile and restriction). To represent every possible railway track on HMI, one must explore different routes and rail networks topologies which are present on the physical location of the railway network of railroads.

Solution:

While exploring various railway track topologies, we have come across the "OpenRailwayMap". This is a web-based tool which provides a detailed map of the world's railway infrastructure including complex railway topologies such as Teardrop where a switch branches off to a loop and merges back to the same line in the opposite direction or Crossing where one track crosses over another.

Area Traffic Control system (ATCS)

Jayant Wagh

We will take a small recap of active traffic controlling methods which we have seen in my last articles.

We have seen that having a Traffic signal is the fastest and most economical method to control traffic at a single or simple intersection. Which are typically operating in the "Fixed time plan" i.e. if there is 4 leg intersection we typically allocate 30 -40 sec Green time depending on the traffic load of each leg.

This type of operation will not be sustainable for a longer time. After a 3 to 5 years span as traffic increases the effectiveness of such a “Fix time signal plan” system gets deteriorated.

Then we have also seen that with the help of a "Traffic detector," we started to detect traffic with a little bit more details like Vehicle Type / Count / Speed, etc. This gives a push to dynamically adjust traffic signal plans as per traffic behavior which we called ATMS "Adaptive Traffic Management System". This system is still limited to one junction only.

Here we are not considering the traffic situation at the adjacent junction or nearby area so travelers may get consecutive “RED” signals while passing such a corridor or area which may lead to traffic jams though we have signals and traffic detectors are in place.

Here coordination between two or more junctions in a particular area or corridor is a key issue. This issue can be solved using the new generation's latest software and communication technology.

Assume that every intersection is connected to a central server and shares detectors/traffic flow data. Server applications are very intelligent and take collaborative decisions which leads to ease of traffic. This evolution gives us better control over one "Area" instead of one intersection, this technology/ method is called ATCS "Area Traffic Control System".

Let’s see the high-level architecture of such an ATCS system , every intersection is connected to central server via a communication network.

This ATCS system has major 4 building blocks

  • Vehicle / Traffic Detectors
  • Intersection Controller
  • Communication Network
  • ATCS- server software

Vehicle detectors

Vehicle Detectors are used to detect the presence of vehicles, average speed, vehicle flow, vehicle density, and queue length measurement.

Detectors act as a nodal point between the vehicle and the intersection controller. The detector could be of various types of example-ultrasonic, microwave radar, infrared laser radar, non-imaging passive infrared, video imaging, acoustic array, magnetic loop Inductive loop vehicle detector is commonly used.

Some special types of detectors are also used to identify the type of vehicle i.e if Vehicle is a public transport/emergency vehicle then ATCS can give priority to these vehicles.

Intersection Signal Controller

This is a small box kept at every intersection, this is nothing but a micro-macro computer with some storage and data processing capability.

The main role of this device is to operate traffic lights as per the given traffic signal plan. .

In ATCS mode, the signal controller also collects traffic detector information and sends it to ATCS with the help of a communication network. This data will be processed by ATCS and send back an updated or optimized signal plan to the controller for on-field operation. This cycle repeats to achieve improved and smooth traffic operation.

Communication network

Communication networks play a very critical role here, this network might be GPRS / Fiber connectivity to a centralized ATCS center. As shown in the figure this communication network is responsible for collecting all detector data Via the Traffic signal controller and sending it to the ATCS center and getting processed data/decision data that is updated signal plans and given to the Signal controller.
