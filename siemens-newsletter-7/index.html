Siemens

The new normal after COVID is in everybody’s mind. Also, in our COBRA management team, we are frequently discussing how this new normal can and should look like. For sure, the old nine to six, five days a week in office will not return post-COVID. Especially in our organization, we learned what can be done without being in the office at all and without meeting colleagues in person.

However, although the operational interaction is going smoothly working from remote, the lack of real social interaction will likely be taking its toll in the long run. I still believe that you can only develop a real bonding and team spirit if colleagues meet in person at least once in a while and a bit more in the beginning.

Therefore, the COBRA management team took the initiative and held the first Cobra line management face-to-face workshop ever in Bengaluru from July 29 to 30. I have to admit that I was really excited to see most of my managers for the first time after more than a year. Sanjay and Anuj I knew from the past, but Sakthi, Raij and Sai I have never met before. I couldn’t remember when it was the last time that I wore a shirt. Others said it took them ages to pack their bag for these 2 days because things that were at hand all the time in the past now had disappeared somewhere in drawers because they were not used anymore.

But it was not just about meeting face to face. We had many topics to discuss, mostly related to our staff. Again we discussed possible concepts for the new normal. The employee survey results and how to address them properly were also on the agenda, just to mention a few topics. Some of the results will be

presented during our upcoming online management information session. We also had the opportunity to meet and interact with the project team of BMRCL, and we appreciate their efforts despite the pandemic and the current challenges in the project.

Overall it was a great experience after such a long time, and I hope that all of us will be getting the opportunity to meet in person soon.

Understanding Excellence

Shrikant Vachaspati,
HR Business Partner Manager, STSPL

I was facilitating an interac- tive session with school going students under the banner of ‘Shikshak Prab- hodhini’ – a project where teachers are being trained to mentor exceptional students. We were discuss- ing ‘emotions’. A young girl of eighth standard, an aspir- ing singer, was discussing with me her emotions before a performance, in front of about 80 other students. We talked about the ‘Result’ and the ‘Process’. The ‘result’ was the end of the performance when the audience would clap and appreciate. The ‘Process’ was the actual perfor- mance that would lead to this result.

  • 18 Likes
  • 10 Likes

We concluded that before starting the actual performance we need to make the ‘result’ ‘out of focus’ that is blurred and bring the actual performance in sharp focus ... by sharp focus we meant, we will now focus on every minute of the performance... minute by minute. The girl visualized aloud the exact moment of starting the performance. She described it as if in a trance, I was helping her to shape her responses ... Here she was, as steady as a rock ... breathing rhythmically.. Image of her guru in front of her eyes ... and the only overriding thought, I am going to do my best...

“And who are you? ... what are you? ... at this moment? ...”

“I am music ...” pat came the reply

“A moment of excellence is born” I said.

It was a very strong experience emotionally for all of us pres- ent. A rare, delightful insight into an experience called ‘excellence’ ... It was exhilarating in an enlightened manner. At times, the experiential truth is so strong that is difficult to catch it in words, however, if one has to consolidate the emotional insight into a blinding design of wisdom, one has to fall back on words again.

So what is excellence?

I do not think excellence has one single definition. In the future segments of this communication, I will explore different ways by which this theme can be understood. Here is the first one.

Let us take for example, a popular skill amongst students such as ‘orator’s skill’. Translated in school-reality, it means participating and winning elocution and debat- ing competitions.

A student that is generally chosen by teachers for developing this skill is an exprand communicative one who also has a ‘pleasant’ and ‘smart ‘disposition’. Suppose there is an elocution competition and five topics are given, out of which she has to select one. The mentor here will ask her to think and talk about each one of the topics ... what are the thoughts that come to her mind when she reads topics such as , ‘Protecting My Earth’ or ‘The Life and Times of Shivaji Maharaj’ . She is encouraged to explore each topic; in her own way. She has to just go on talking about each topic. The mentor listens keenly.

“Can you now make a choice?” the mentor asks the question unconstructively. She needs to give her own explanation why she is deciding to opt for a particular topic. If she is confused, the mentor helps her to explore her confusion (please note, not to clear it).

When she makes a choice she has to give justification for it. Having heard that; the mentor poses certain relevant queries regarding her choice. What is the most important aspect of the topic that she has liked? What according to her is the part of the topic that she may find difficult? If she has ‘liked’ two topics, what is one consideration that will help her to choose the one that she has chosen?

At the end, the mentor and student will decide a time interval where the student has to think of all the points raised and come back with a decision.

“But sir, I have already made up my mind” suppose the student says.

“Yes, I appreciate ... I want you now to tell me one by one why

and how you discarded the other topics. I am interested in knowing how you did it...”

“How did I do it? I just did it” student. “Let us explore how you could have done it... I am taking a paper and writing about all topics. I want you to write all the thoughts that came to your mind ... that could help ...”

The mentor helps the student to identify consciously the thinking process that went behind her decision. The topic is now selected.

The mentor now motivates the student to gather information on the topic. The mentor shares the spirit of enquiry, exploration and excitement. Having an adequate ‘database’ the mentor helps the student to evolve the structure of presentation. The structure is not imposed. Any deviation from standard procedure of delivering a speech, is respected and discussed.

  • 18 Likes
  • 10 Likes

The mundane use of quotations, use of lofty words, adult and literary phrases is questioned. The originality of expression is encouraged. A draft is now ready.

The mentor and the student now discuss the style of presentation. The mentor does not impose his or any other’s style on the student. They make a list of ‘impressive orators’ that they have observed in media as well as in public life. The student makes note of the qualities that she would like to imbibe from each one of them. The written draft is taken as a guideline rather than a script.

While rehearsing the speech, placement of all phrases and points is discussed. The contents are rearranged, realigned and the impact is noted. Then the student is given different scenarios and told to present the speech

  • Student is the first speaker
  • Last speaker
  • All the points covered by the previous speaker
  • Distracted audience
  • Tired judges
  • Interruption because of electricity / technical faults.

Each performance is discussed.

Then the ‘emotions’ during preparation and rehearsal are discussed. Boredom, anxiety, satisfaction...Each emotion is named and each emotion is acknowledged. The emotions that will help the performance are identified. How can the student continue to generate these helpful emotions? ... different strategies are planned by which this can be done.

The issue of ‘ownership’ of performance is now discussed. This ‘ownership’ can be burdening or enjoyable, the way you look at it. On the day of the performance, the mentor and the student spend a short ‘exclusive time’ together. After the performance the mentor and the student discuss pros and cons of her own performance. Rather than saying “your delivery was too fast”, it is put as “These were the sentences where your speed was fast” Rather than saying “you need to work on your eye-to-eye contact” it is put as, “you need to periodically cover all corners of the audience by your eyes”.

After the performance is discussed, both mentor and student sincerely sit through some other contestants’ speeches and discuss those. Slowly they evolve a detailed format of performance appraisal which is kept as a record by the student. After the result of the competition, the mentor explores resultant emotions with the student. They decide on their next goal.

As the time progresses, the student starts becoming more responsible and empowered and the mentor maintains a meaningful presence through- out this journey.

The student now grows confident and starts exploring the experience further. The mentor now focuses on ‘involvement’, ‘commitment’, ‘process satisfaction’ and ‘result satisfaction’ which are psychologically more sophisticated terms.

The discussion revolves around, what is the ‘self-talk’ at such times and how different types of self-talk can be either helpful or harmful; how the student can exercise her choice to choose the most helpful self-talk.

As the string of performances continues and each is utilized as a ‘learning’ experience, there will come an experience the mentor is watching for... a memorable moment when the student and the speech being delivered becomes so synchronized that it becomes ONE.

The student is at her best at that moment. This moment is again shared and explored by both. It is a magical moment. A touch of brilliance. Now, the mentor and the student keep on working further to make this moment appear often as well as how they can make it ‘better’. Here better, means more meaning- ful, more memorable, more intense…

And slowly, the moments of excellence start trickling... then flowing... Naturally ... In a rhythm. The student has discovered her own way of expression. The mentor watches with his ever-watchful eyes her track-record of glories with moist eyes, with all the memories gathering one by one in his mind... “There was a day when I helped her to make her choice” he says in his mind and smiles.

The Business Excellence (BE) function @ ADV MO consists of 3 constellations / teams dedicated to drive process excellence, compliance, safety and validation and agile practices. It comprises Quality Management, Siemens Development System (SDS), and RAMS teams. BE truly combines the expertise of all three key areas which in turn enables us to achieve our business goals.

Let us have a look at what each team does:

Quality Management (QM) Team:

This team comprises Quality Managers or QMiPs who are experts in process definition, implementation of best practices and process improvement. They work closely with projects as QMiPs or Verifiers in projects to ensure key process and compliance requirements are understood by all team members and are seamlessly implemented with the help of GUIDE and IMS. Product quality backed by lean process implementation and improvement, safety verification, root cause analysis, and data analysis is the prime focus. QM team also ensures that the organization complies to all required standards and certifications such as:

  • ISO 9000 (Quality Management System),
  • ISO 27000 (Information Security Management System),
  • ISO 14000 (Environment Management System)
  • ISO 45000 (Organization Health and Safety)
  • CMMI V2.0 at Level 3

Our key offerings are:

  • Facilitation provided to projects for process implementation based on GUIDE/ IMS as per Safety norms.
  • Improve processes and performance in projects
  • Analyze project and organization metrics data and derive meaningful insights
  • Governance in projects through Project Status Review meetings and actions, and through audits
  • Facilitate process trainings, RCA, Lessons Learned workshops
  • Conduct Safety Verification activities
  • Collaborate with Central Quality Team at ADV Level & GUIDE Core Team at SMO RI level

SDS Team:

Siemens Development System (SDS) team offers training, coaching and consultations across Mobility and other Siemens groups. This team is actively involved in implementation of Agile practices using best practices under SDS framework.

SDS team has the following offerings:
  • SDS and SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) Trainings
  • Coaching, Mentoring and Facilitation
  • Project Coaching: SDS Team enables individual project teams adopt best of Lean/Agile practices in their agile journey.
  • SDS Reflection which is a facilitated self-health check of a scrum team’s agile journey

RAMS

Reliability, Availability, Maintainability, and Safety (RAMS) is a specialist discipline & constitutes the key elements of the assessment in the Rail Industry today. RAMS means a safe, reliable, high-quality service offering with lower operating and maintenance costs. RAMS is the basis of many companies’ competitive edge. It strives to improve railway operational efficiency by ensuring appropriate processes are in place to monitor performance and take proactive steps towards addressing emerging problems.

We assure that our systems are:

  • Available & achieve Availability targets e.g. 99.5% of availability
  • Maintainable & achieve maintainability targets e.g. Mean Time to Repair < X
  • SAFE & achieve all the safety targets either Quantitative or Qualitative.
  • Approve for Independent Safety Assessment (ISA) certification for use on Railway.
  • Validated for its alignment with the specifications & compliant to CENELEC standards (EN 50126/128/129).

#InThisTogether

Project teams require a supportive environment of process set-up, agility, system checks, safety and compliance assurance for a seamless delivery.

This is where all the three teams in BE come together to enable the project delivery. Firstly, the QM team helps to set-up the base of processes for safety/non-safety based on Safety Integrity Levels, project scope, and ensures the adherence leading to good quality product. The SDS team coaches the project teams to add value delivery through discovery of best practices during execution with built-in quality and faster delivery. Finally, the RAMS team helps in validating the safety requirements right from definition of requirements to understanding how the product meets the agreed safety norms at the end of lifecycle.

We have come together to assist in delivering world class products and in this journey, we have slowly built our competency through collaborative learnings and will continuously strive to improve our competencies and overall organizational capabilities.
