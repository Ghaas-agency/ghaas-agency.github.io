Vehicle-Actuated Signals

Traffic congestion is becoming a critical issue day by day, and this is due to the increase in urbanization, industries, and vehicles. Sometimes emergency vehicles like ambulances and fire trucks are stuck in traffic and unable to reach in time for rescue.

The worst traffic jams or long queues occur at intersections where cars, bikes, buses, or pedestrians cross the road. If we can manage traffic at such intersections, we might resolve this issue significantly. Almost all big cities have traffic signals and a dedicated traffic police force for managing everyday traffic. In case of emergencies, the police can use a traffic control panel to make dynamic signal changes or take control over intersection traffic manually.

"Vehicle-Actuated Signals" is one of the preferred methods for optimizing intersection traffic. There are also various detectors used for actuation. e.g., inductive loop detectors, radar, RFID, or cameras.

Signal controller programs take these detector actuations as input from all approach lanes and dynamically adjust the signal timing plan and reduce queue length on all lanes as much as possible. These steps help reduce congestion time significantly. Some cities have preferred special vehicle detection mechanisms to detect emergency vehicles and public transport vehicles to prioritize cross intersections.

Typically, traffic signal controllers operate in three modes.

Pre-Timed Signal: In this mode, a traffic signal's Green-Amber-Red timing is fixed, and these patterns are repeated throughout the day. Sometimes, these fixed timing partners or designs are based on historical traffic scenarios. An example can be giving more time to the green signals in the morning, especially those that lead to offices or industrial areas. This will ensure the smooth movement of traffic.

Actuated Signal: In this method, one can use vehicle detectors to detect traffic based on queue length, vehicle type, and priority; based on these factors, signal controllers can optimize signal timing dynamically. Taking the same scenario as above, if there is more traffic moving towards the office or industrial area, a lane could get maximum green time dynamically.

Mixed-Mode: One can use this mode at major and minor traffic road junctions. Typically, major roads have fixed timing, and minor roads get actuated signals. By doing this, minor roads will get a green signal when there is sizable traffic. Otherwise, you end up prioritizing just major-road traffic.

Advantages of Actuated Signals

  • They can reduce congestion/delay (if properly timed).
  • They are adaptable to short-term fluctuations in traffic flow.
  • They can provide continuous operation under low volume conditions.
  • They are especially useful at multiple phase intersections.

Disadvantages of Actuated Signals

  • If the traffic demand pattern is very regular, the extra benefit of adding local actuation is minimal or non-existent.
  • Installation cost is two to three times the cost of a pre-timed signal installation.
  • Actuated controllers are much more complicated than pre-timed controllers, increasing maintenance costs.
  • They require careful inspection & maintenance to ensure proper operation.

Traffic engineers are coming up with newer concepts like Signal Synchronization, which allows coordinating traffic signals along a single route so that vehicles get a progressive green signal at each junction. This is another crucial aspect that we will discuss more in the upcoming newsletter.

Magnification in train control system

Metro Signalling & Train control system technology focus means of reducing headway, ensuring safety, and improving efficiency and reduction in the cost of operation. The Signalling & Train Control system on Main Line Railways and Metros are different on account of very different traffic requirements besides civil and E&M infrastructure. Signalling & Train Control in metros not only ensures train safety, but it also integrates, interfaces, and automates areas of operation, driving, passenger information, collection of information, and analysis of the same. Signalling & Train Control systems have been dominated using Track Circuit-based Signalling & Train Control for more than five decades.

The success of Metro rail systems lies in their ability to provide frequent, fast, safe, and comfortable journeys in the urban conglomeration, not only to the regular commuters but also to the occasional traveler or tourist alike. Signalling and Train Control systems play a major role in achieving these objectives. While enhancing operational efficiency by providing control systems to achieve the target headway, it also always ensures the total safety of the train movements besides adding value to other systems by the on-line interface.

The rapid developments in the computer and microcircuit technology, as well as telecommunication, have led to the development of Integrated Metro Control systems, in which computers (including failsafe forms of computers) fulfill many of the decision making roles traditionally performed by Train operators, station controllers, Traffic controllers, etc., eventually leading to the introduction of highly automated Metro Rail with driverless trains.

Need for Train detection and methods used:

To convey information to a train about where to stop and at what speed to run required all the trains on any line to be detected automatically by the system. This was achieved by the invention of the so-called ‚Äútrack circuits‚Äù. Track circuits were first invented in the USA (DC Track circuits) in 1872 and later picked up and improved by British railways. London Underground was the first large-scale user in a Metro rail network 1904-06.

The need to provide information to train resulted in the invention of ‚Äúcoded track circuits‚Äù with modulation of a basic AC carrier with codes. These required insulated joints between sections of rails like earlier track circuits for demarcating the individual track circuit blocks. Later came to the ‚Äújointless track circuits‚Äù which use audio frequencies and provide high impedance between adjacent track circuits by a tuning process thus avoiding the need for insulated joints. These are commonly called Audio frequency Track Circuits (AFTC) and these help in transmitting codes that modulate the basic AFTC carrier which in turn are picked up by an antenna suspended below the train.

In addition to track circuits, another system called ‚Äúaxle counter‚Äù is also used for train detection. In this method, axles are detected through a magnetic circuit linking a transmitter and a receiver across the rail, and whenever a metallic axle interposes the received signal drops are detected and counted as one pulse. In this manner, all the axles coming into one block are counted at the entry and if all the axles are counted out at the exit of the block the block is declared as clear.

In the latest advancement in Metro signalling, a system called Communication based train control (CBTC) has been in use for more than a decade. In this system, the primary train detection is purely based on a failsafe communication link between the train and the control center with the train communicating its position continuously and the control Centre communicates the position to other trains for maintaining the safety distance between two trains. Track circuits or axle counters if any are used only as a secondary detection in case the Metro operator desires a fallback system.

Characteristics of Communication-Based Train Control System (CBTC)

Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC) is a railway signalling system that makes use of the telecommunications between the train and track equipment for traffic management and train control. CBTC systems enable, the exact position of a train is known more accurately than with the traditional signalling systems, which results more efficiently and safely to manage the railway traffic and improve headway.

CBTC systems allow different levels of automation or Grades of Automation, GoA, as classified in the IEC 62290-1. CBTC is not a synonym for ‚Äúdriverless‚Äù or ‚Äúautomated trains‚Äù although it is considered as a basic technology for this purpose.

Driverless Train Operation (DTO) and Unattended Train Operation (UTO): More sophisticated systems free the driver from the need to be at the front of the train ‚Äì referred to as Driverless Train Operation (DTO) or GOA level 3. In DTO the driver can move away from the front of the train but remains available to provide customer-facing duties and to drive the train in the event of a failure of the DTO/ATO system.

As the driver is no longer able to see the route ahead this imposes a greater demand on guideway security and platform controls. Platform screen doors must be provided, to allow ATO to handle door closing also on the signal from ATP that all the Platform Screen Doors have closed fully.

In DTO, train doors and train departure from a station platform may also be controlled automatically or manually from a location other than a driver cab at the front of the train. ATO can be given the operating instructions by the ATS regarding dwell time and hold for regulating the train on platforms as required by the operational situation and in the normal course, the driver wherever he happens to be on the train is relieved of all operational workload.

CCTV information of the inside of coaches is transmitted live in new systems to OCC for controllers to observe for any untoward incidents inside the moving train and with other advancements in prevention and detection of unauthorized access to the viaduct or tunnel, even a separate train operator is no longer required on the cab in the train. The space required for the equipment could also be saved and passenger capacity increased at the end cabs. Thus, it became possible to have an Unattended Train Operation. Driverless ATO without an on-board attendant is referred to as Unattended Train Operation (UTO) or GOA level 4. UTO can range from empty train movements ( only to a siding, or in an automated depot, for example) to the operation of trains in passenger service with no attendant on board. The latter requires that the reliability of the systems has to be of a higher level and the train can be operated remotely under failure conditions, or at the minimum can be reached by shore-based personal in a short period. Passengers need to be reassured and hence good communication and CCTV links between the vehicle and an informed staff member at OCC or a specified location are essential.

Benefits of CBTC

CBTC makes effective use of infrastructure in the following ways

  • Allows trains at closer headways.
  • Greater precision and control of trains is possible.
  • Provides continuous safe train separation & over-speed protection.
  • Utilizes the track most efficiently while ensuring safety.

CBTC requires less maintenance cost due to:

  • Driverless system (or upgradable to driverless) to reduce operating costs.
  • Less wayside equipment
  • Improved reliability
  • Real-time diagnostic data

Risks of CBTC

The primary risk of a CBTC system is that if the communications link between any of the trains is disrupted then all or part of the system might have to enter a failsafe state until the problem is remedied. Depending on the severity of the communication loss, this state can range from vehicles temporarily reducing speed, coming to a halt, or operating in a degraded mode until communications are re-established.

Communications failures can result from equipment malfunction, electromagnetic interference, weak signal strength or saturation of the communications medium. In this case, an interruption can result in a service brake or emergency brake application as real-time situational awareness is a critical safety requirement for CBTC and if these interruptions are frequent enough it could seriously impact service.

Cyber-physical challenge is the severe risk in this system. CBTC Systems that make use of open standards for wireless digital communications link have a much larger attack surface and can be subject to various types of hacking including intrusion of the communications network and tampering with safety-critical messages that, in the worst case, could result in a safety hazard.

CBTC systems may be designed with centralized supervision systems, to improve maintainability and reduce installation costs. If so, there is an increased risk of a single point of failure that could disrupt service over an entire system or line.

Talevo: Competency Management System

Imagine an employee struggling to deliver a project due to it requiring new skills and being unaware of whom to consult for technical issues. Think from the perspective of a manager of a team who has worked hard to ensure delivery despite skill gaps. Could he eliminate those risks or get the right people/expert help at the right time? And thus, the Talevo journey started for SMO DT IT HUB in India.

Talevo is an in-house developed competency management system based on CENELAC standard and Master competency definition. It provides a clear framework of skills, application knowledge, and (behavior) abilities that employees must meet to fulfill their job expectations and contribute to the company's success. This product is developed to ensure competencies are captured, tracked, and updated in a transparent and easy way to help the site, department, and employees succeed. Talevo is an instrument to uncover gaps in the current state and empower users for quicker decisions to realize the organization‚Äôs vision.

The rollout for Talevo in SMO DT IT HUB started on November 1st, 2020, and the date of handover to operations was decided as 15th February 2021. Within these timelines, all the modules comprising employee profile management, competency-based employee search, competency management, and competency review were scheduled to be completed. The HUB management decided to delegate the responsibility of Talevo Rollout to two project managers, Bhunesh Bhaskar & Abhishek Tripathi, from the Infrastructure Services team headed by Samarth Karnekar.

Since both these project managers had recently joined the organization, it was a great opportunity for them to showcase their project management skills. This rollout also gave them an opportunity to connect with their colleagues and team managers from different verticals within the HUB. Dividing all the activities into parallel work packages and adherence to timelines meant that the rollout was completed well within the given timeline. Post the successful implementation of this competency management system other business units and teams have started adopting the tool with the help of SMO DT IT HUB as a role model.
